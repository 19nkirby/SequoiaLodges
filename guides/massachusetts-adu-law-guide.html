The Massachusetts ADU Law Guide

In February 2025 the Affordable Homes Act took effect, and with it the single biggest obstacle to building an accessory dwelling unit in Massachusetts disappeared: for most single-family lots, you no longer need a variance or special permit. This guide covers what changed, whether your property qualifies, and what a realistic path from idea to move-in looks like.

01What actually changed

  • ADUs up to 900 sq ft, or 50% of the main home's gross floor area (whichever is smaller), are now allowed by right in single-family zoning districts statewide.
  • "By right" means no variance, no special permit, no zoning board hearing for a qualifying ADU. Local zoning that conflicts with the state law is no longer enforceable.
  • Municipalities cannot require owner-occupancy as a condition of a by-right ADU.
  • Parking: at most one space may be required — and none at all if the property is within half a mile of a commuter rail, subway, ferry, or bus station.
  • You still need the normal approvals every project needs: a standard building permit, septic (Title 5) or sewer sign-off, and a Certificate of Occupancy. Reasonable local rules on setbacks, height, and site design still apply — they just can't be used to block a qualifying ADU outright.
  • A note on Boston: Boston runs its own citywide ADU zoning under Article 53, with its own dimensional standards and BPDA review. The practical upshot is the same — most Boston homeowners now have a permitted, variance-free path to an ADU. Our plan sets are designed to both standards.

02Am I eligible? The 2-minute checklist

If you can tick most of these boxes, your property very likely qualifies for a by-right ADU.

  • My property is in a single-family zoning district (or in Boston, where Article 53 applies citywide).
  • The ADU I have in mind is 900 sq ft or less, and no more than half the size of my main house. Example: a 1,600 sq ft home supports a by-right ADU up to 800 sq ft.
  • There's a workable spot — backyard, side yard, garage footprint, or basement — that keeps reasonable distance from property lines.
  • My lot isn't in a special overlay that adds review (historic district, conservation/wetlands buffer, flood zone). These don't kill a project, but they add steps.
  • If I'm on septic: my system has (or can be upgraded to have) capacity for an additional bedroom count.
  • I'm the owner of record, or under contract to buy.

05How homeowners are financing ADUs in 2026

  • Home equity (HELOC or home-equity loan) — the most common route; MA homeowners are sitting on substantial post-2020 equity.
  • Cash-out refinance — makes sense mainly for owners whose existing rate is already near current market rates.
  • Renovation / construction loans — lend against the home's future value with the ADU, useful when current equity is thin.
  • State & local programs — check MassHousing and your municipality; several Massachusetts communities have piloted ADU loan or technical-assistance programs, and offerings change year to year.
  • Rental offset — if the ADU will be rented, some lenders now count projected rental income toward qualification. Ask directly; policies vary widely.

06Questions to ask before hiring anyone

  • Have you designed (or built) ADUs under the new state law and/or Boston's Article 53 specifically?
  • Is your price fixed, and exactly what does it include — structural engineering? Stamped drawings? Compliance documentation?
  • Who handles site-specific adjustments — setbacks, utility routing, grading — and is that included?
  • What do you need from me (survey, plot plan) and what happens if my lot has a complication?
  • For contractors: can I see a recent ADU project and talk to that homeowner?
  • Who pulls the permit, and who responds if the building department has review comments?
