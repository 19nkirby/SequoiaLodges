Greater Boston ADU Permit-Readiness Checklist

Answer these questions before you spend money on design. If you can tick most of the boxes, your project is ready to move fast. If you can't yet, you now know exactly what to chase down — and none of it requires hiring anyone.

01Lot & access

  • I know my lot dimensions and total lot area (even approximately).
  • There is a spot on the lot — backyard, side yard, or garage footprint — that could fit a structure of roughly 400–900 sq ft.
  • That spot keeps a reasonable distance from the property lines. Setback rules vary by municipality; in Boston, Article 53 governs ADU dimensional standards.
  • Equipment could physically reach the build site (a path or driveway at least a few feet wide, or alley access).
  • I know whether any easements, shared driveways, or protected trees affect the buildable area.
  • The site doesn't sit in an obvious problem zone — steep slope, ledge outcrop, or standing water after rain. These don't necessarily kill a project, but they change the engineering.

02Survey & site plan

  • I have (or know how to get) a plot plan or survey of my property. Check your closing documents — many homeowners already have one from purchase or refinance.
  • The survey shows the existing house, lot lines, and any accessory structures.
  • If I don't have one, I've identified a local surveyor and asked for a quote. This is the single most common cause of timeline delays — order it early.

03Utilities

  • I know where my water and sewer lines run (or that my property is on septic).
  • My electrical panel has capacity for an additional dwelling, or I've noted that a service upgrade may be needed.
  • I've thought about how heat will be provided — most new ADUs use electric mini-splits, which avoid extending gas service.
  • Water/sewer connection points are on the same side of the lot as the proposed ADU, or I've flagged the longer run.

04Documentation & approvals

  • I've confirmed I'm the owner of record (or am under contract to buy).
  • I know which municipality's rules apply, and whether my property is in a historic or conservation district.
  • I've looked up my city's ADU page. For Boston: the BPDA ADU program and Article 53. Cambridge, Newton, Brookline and others each have their own ordinances.
  • I know whether my lot is owner-occupied per my city's ADU requirements, where applicable.
  • I've checked whether my mortgage or insurance carrier needs to be notified of a second dwelling.
